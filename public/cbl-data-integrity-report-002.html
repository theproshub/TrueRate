1. Executive Summary

During routine data validation on July 1, 2026, TrueRate's automated quality-assurance system identified three distinct anomalies across the Interest Rates (INR), Government Finance (FIS), and Balance of Payments (BOP) databanks published through the CBL DataWarehousePro API.

These anomalies affect a combined 16 series and involve data that is mathematically inconsistent with the hierarchical relationships defined by the DataWarehousePro schema. In each case, the error is detectable algorithmically and likely originates from data-entry or data-processing procedures.

Summary of Findings Finding 1: All six LRD/USD interest rate pairs report identical values for February 2026, despite maintaining distinct currency spreads in prior months. This is economically implausible in a dual-currency banking system.

Finding 2: In March 2026, Salary & Wages (a child of Recurrent Expenditure) is recorded at US$32.24 million — 77% larger than its parent category of US$18.17 million. A sub-component cannot exceed its total.

Finding 3: In Q4 2025, Workers' remittances (US$199.76 million) exceeds its parent category, Secondary Income Debit Outflows (US$48.04 million), by a factor of 4.2x. A "of which" memo item cannot exceed the total it belongs to.

2. Finding 1 — Interest Rate LRD/USD Column Duplication

Critical — 12 series affected

2.1 Description

The CBL publishes industry-average interest rates for six product categories, each broken out by currency denomination (Liberian Dollar and US Dollar). In a dual-currency economy, LRD-denominated rates are expected to differ from USD-denominated rates because lenders price in currency risk, liquidity premiums, and different funding costs. A persistent, economically meaningful spread between the two is normal and expected.

In the February 2026 observation, all six LRD/USD pairs report exactly identical values. In prior months, these pairs maintained distinct spreads. The simultaneous collapse of all six spreads to zero in a single month has no economic precedent and is inconsistent with the mechanics of currency-specific lending.

2.3 Analysis

The pattern is consistent with a column-copy error during data entry: the values for one currency denomination were pasted into the column for the other. The four product categories that had distinct spreads in January (Lending, Savings, Time Deposit, CDs) all lost those spreads simultaneously in February, while the two categories that were already coincidentally equal (Mortgage, Personal Loan) remained so.

We also note that the Mortgage and Personal Loan rates have been identical across LRD and USD for the entire six-month window available (September 2025 through February 2026). While it is theoretically possible that banks charge the same rate regardless of currency for these specific products, we recommend verifying this with the underlying banking survey data, as it may reflect a longer-running entry issue for these two series.

Determining which column is correct We cannot determine from the published data alone whether the LRD values were copied into the USD column or vice versa. The source banking survey worksheets would resolve this. For the four pairs where a clear spread existed in January 2026, the January values provide a reference for what the correct February values should approximate.

3. Finding 2 — Government Budget: Parent Smaller Than Child

Critical — 3 series affected

3.1 Description

The Government Finance (FIS) databank organizes budget data in a parent-child hierarchy. Recurrent Expenditure (LBR_FIS_BUD_2_1) is a sub-category of Total Expenditure, and its own children include Salary & Wages (LBR_FIS_BUD_2_1_1) and Loans, Interest & Other Charges (LBR_FIS_BUD_2_1_2). By definition, the sum of all children must be less than or equal to the parent.

In the March 2026 observation, this accounting identity is violated: the child component Salary & Wages alone exceeds its parent Recurrent Expenditure.

3.2 Evidence

SeriesMnemonicJan-26Feb-26Mar-26
Recurrent Expenditure (parent) LBR_FIS_BUD_2_1 26.54 51.04 18.17
 Salary & Wages (child) LBR_FIS_BUD_2_1_1 9.95 19.76 32.24
 Loans, Interest & Other (child) LBR_FIS_BUD_2_1_2 6.55 4.53 11.15
Sum of children (Mar-26) 43.39 M USD > Parent 18.17 M USD

All values are in Million USD. In January and February 2026, the parent correctly exceeds the sum of its children (with the residual representing other recurrent items not broken out). In March 2026, this relationship inverts.

3.3 Analysis

The March 2026 values suggest either:

  1. Swapped values: The parent and child figures may have been transposed during entry. If Recurrent Expenditure were 32.24 and Salary & Wages were 18.17, the identity would hold (with a residual of 3.02 for other recurrent items), which is structurally plausible.
  2. Incorrect Salary & Wages entry: The 32.24 figure is a 3.2x increase from the prior month (9.95 in Jan-26), which is unusual for a government payroll that is relatively stable month-to-month.

Additionally, the Recurrent Expenditure figure of 18.17 in March is the lowest value in the three-month window, despite historically being the largest budget line item. This further suggests the value is misplaced.

4. Finding 3 — BOP: Sub-Item Exceeds Parent Category

Critical — 2 series affected

4.1 Description

In the Balance of Payments (BOP) databank, Workers' remittances (LBR_BOP_1_7_2_1) is defined as a "of which" memo item under Secondary Income — Debit (Outflows) (LBR_BOP_1_7_2). By definition, a memo sub-item cannot exceed the total it belongs to.

In the most recent three quarters, the Workers' remittances figure exceeds the Debit Outflows total by a factor of approximately 4x.

4.2 Evidence

All values are in Million USD.

4.3 Analysis

The violation is persistent across all three available quarters, not a one-off data-entry error. This suggests a structural issue in how the series hierarchy is defined or how the data is compiled. Possible explanations:

  1. Hierarchy mis-assignment: Workers' remittances (LBR_BOP_1_7_2_1) may be incorrectly nested under Debit Outflows. In the BPM6 framework, workers' remittances typically appear under the Credit (Inflows) side of Secondary Income (LBR_BOP_1_7_1), not the Debit side. If the series is re-mapped under Credit Inflows, the relationship would be mathematically consistent — Q4 2025 Credit Inflows would need to be verified.
  2. Incorrect parent value: The Debit Outflows figure (~48M USD) may be understated. If the true debit outflows including remittances exceed 200M USD, the parent figure is too low.
Most Likely Explanation Workers' remittances are typically a credit (inflow) to the receiving economy. Liberia is a net recipient of remittances from the large diaspora. The series LBR_BOP_1_7_2_1 is most likely mis-classified under Debit (Outflows) when it should be under Credit (Inflows). The values themselves (~US$200M/quarter) are consistent with published estimates of Liberia's annual remittance inflows.

5. Additional Anomalies Requiring Verification

In addition to the three definitive errors above, our automated audit flagged the following anomalies. These may represent legitimate economic events, seasonal adjustments, or data-processing artifacts. We present them for verification against source documents rather than as confirmed errors.

5.1 Government Budget — Extreme Month-over-Month Movements (Mar-26)

High — verify with Ministry of Finance

Two Government Finance series exhibit month-over-month changes that exceed plausible bounds for government flow variables:

SeriesMnemonicFeb-26Mar-26Change
Non-Tax Revenue LBR_FIS_BUD_1_2 17.15 M USD 244.91 M USD +1,328%
Capital Expenditure LBR_FIS_BUD_2_2 0.06 M USD 49.68 M USD +82,700%

Non-Tax Revenue: A 14x increase in a single month (from US$17.15M to US$244.91M) is unprecedented in the available history. The prior six months ranged from US$10–25M. If legitimate, this would represent an extraordinary one-time receipt (e.g. a mining concession payment or donor disbursement) that warrants annotation in the data.

Capital Expenditure: An increase from US$0.06M to US$49.68M represents an 82,700% change. The near-zero February value (US$0.06M) is itself suspect — capital spending rarely falls to effectively zero. This pattern suggests either a data-entry error or a timing artifact where expenditures were not recorded in February and were batch-entered in March.

Three Central Bank Survey (CBS) series show movements that are difficult to reconcile with normal monetary operations:

Net Foreign Assets: The CBS NFA swung from -3,736M LRD in January to +43,179M LRD in March — a sign change followed by a 12x increase. While an SDR allocation or large foreign-currency transaction could cause an NFA jump, the magnitude (roughly US$225M at current exchange rates) would represent a transformative event for Liberia's central bank balance sheet. We recommend verifying against the monthly balance sheet.

Liabilities to Other Sector: This series spiked from 155M LRD (Oct–Nov 2025 average) to 3,926M LRD in February 2026, then collapsed back to 151M LRD in March. The spike-and-reversal pattern is consistent with a temporary classification error or an end-of-period adjustment that was reversed.

Other Items (Net): The depository corporations survey "Other items (net)" — a residual balancing line — dropped 76% in one month then jumped 456% the next. Large swings in this residual line often indicate reclassifications or valuation adjustments elsewhere in the monetary survey that may not have been applied consistently.

5.3 Production Data — Unit of Measure Error

High — metadata correction

The Crude Palm Oil (CPO) production series (LBR_PRO_3) is labelled with a unit of measure of "Kilograms". Recent monthly values range from 1,149 to 12,612. If these figures were truly in kilograms, Liberia's monthly CPO output would be 1–13 tonnes — implausibly low for a country with over 80,000 hectares of oil palm plantations.

The values are consistent with Metric Tonnes (1,149–12,612 MT/month = 14,000–150,000 MT/year), which aligns with Liberia's published production capacity. The unit label in the series metadata should be corrected from "Kilograms" to "Metric Tonnes".

Note on Data Staleness Multiple key series have not been updated for several months: GDP (17.9 months stale), Balance of Payments (9 months), Government Debt (7 months), and Exchange Rate / CPI / Money Supply (4 months each). Our daily sync cron is running without errors, confirming that the DataWarehousePro API is operational but has not published newer observations for these series. We flag this for awareness as it may indicate a pipeline delay on the publication side.

6. Downstream Impact

The DataWarehousePro API is consumed by multiple data platforms, research institutions, and financial publications. These anomalies can produce the following downstream effects:

  • Interest rate analysis: Analysts and financial publications relying on the DataWarehousePro will incorrectly report that the currency spread between LRD and USD banking products has collapsed to zero, potentially triggering misguided commentary about currency convergence or dollarization.
  • Fiscal monitoring: The inverted parent-child relationship in the government budget data makes it impossible for downstream consumers to correctly compute recurrent spending composition or wage-bill-to-revenue ratios — key indicators used by the IMF, World Bank, and credit rating agencies in their assessments of Liberia's fiscal position.
  • Balance of payments analysis: The remittance classification error overstates debit outflows and understates credit inflows, distorting the secondary income balance and, by extension, the current account balance. For an economy where remittances represent a significant share of GDP, this error has material implications for macroeconomic analysis.
  • Public trust: Inconsistencies between published data and basic accounting identities can undermine confidence in official statistics among researchers, investors, and international institutions.

6.1 Interest Rates (Finding 1)

Proposed Fix Re-source the February 2026 interest rate data from the original banking survey returns. Enter the correct LRD and USD values for each of the six rate categories. Additionally, verify the Mortgage and Personal Loan series for September 2025 through February 2026, as these show persistent zero spreads that may indicate a longer-running entry issue.

The following SQL identifies the affected rows (adapt table and column names to match the DataWarehousePro schema):

8. Prevention Recommendations

To prevent similar errors in future data publications, we respectfully suggest the following automated validation checks be added to the DataWarehousePro ingestion pipeline:

  1. Currency-pair spread check: For any LRD/USD rate pair, flag if both values are identical. A zero spread is economically implausible for currency-differentiated products and should require manual confirmation before publication.
  2. Parent-child accounting identity check: For all hierarchical series (FIS budget items, BOP components), validate at ingestion time that parent ≥ sum(children). Reject the batch if this identity is violated.
  3. Memo-item bound check: For "of which" memo sub-items, validate that memo_item ≤ parent_total for all periods. This is a strict algebraic constraint that should never be violated.
  4. Velocity check: Flag any month-over-month change exceeding ±100% for flow variables (revenue, expenditure) for manual review before publication. The March 2026 salary figure (32.24M, up from 9.95M in January) would have been caught by this rule.
  5. Cross-period consistency: When entering a new month's data, display the prior month's values side-by-side to reduce transposition errors.
